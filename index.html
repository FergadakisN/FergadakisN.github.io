San Francisco Crime: A Short Data Story

This page presents a brief, author-guided story about how reported crime in San Francisco has changed over time and space. All figures are based on the same SF crime dataset used throughout the course.

Introducing the Data

The figures below are based on police incident reports from San Francisco, covering multiple years of recorded crime. Each row in the dataset represents a reported incident, with information about the offense type, time, and location.

Like any administrative dataset, these records reflect both actual events and the way police choose to record and enforce them. When you see a spike or drop in the charts, it might signal a real change in crime, a shift in enforcement priorities, or a mix of both. The goal of this short story is to highlight a few clear patterns rather than explain every detail of the city's crime dynamics.

1. How reported crime evolved over time

We start with a static overview of total reported crime by year. This view smooths out daily noise and lets us see broad shifts in how often incidents are recorded in the city.

2. Where specific offenses cluster in the city

A map helps reveal geography that a time-series alone cannot show. Here, each point (or hotspot) on the map corresponds to locations where a particular offense category is concentrated.

3. Exploring patterns interactively

Finally, an interactive Plotly visualization lets the reader dig into a different slice of the data—for example, comparing categories across districts or exploring how incidents vary by time of day. Use the hover and filter controls in the chart to focus on the categories or areas that interest you most.

What to take away

Taken together, these three views highlight several key patterns in the SF crime data:

  • Many crime categories show a clear break in trend around 2020–2021, suggesting that broader events (like the pandemic or policy changes) reshaped reported crime rather than a smooth continuation of past patterns.
  • Crime is highly uneven across districts: some neighborhoods consistently appear as hotspots for particular offenses, while others remain relatively low across categories.
  • Crime patterns are both temporal and spatial: incident counts shift not only by location but also across years, months, weekdays, and even hours of the day, revealing distinct rhythms for different types of crime.

None of these figures can fully explain why crime changes, but they can point to patterns worth investigating further. Any interpretation should be paired with context about policing, policy, and neighborhood change—otherwise, it is easy to mistake changes in recording for changes in people's behavior.
